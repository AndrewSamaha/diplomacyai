
**Diplomacy** is a strategic board game of negotiation, alliances, and betrayal set in pre–World War I Europe. Each player controls one “Great Power” (like England, France, Germany, Russia, Austria-Hungary, Italy, or Turkey) and commands military units—**armies** (land) and **fleets** (sea).

### What you’re trying to do

The objective is to capture and hold a majority of the map’s **supply centers** (key territories). Holding more supply centers lets you **build more units**, which increases your influence and reach.

### How a turn works

Play happens in repeating seasons/years (e.g., **Spring 1901**, **Fall 1901**, then winter adjustments):

* **Planning & Negotiation (real core of the game):** Players make deals, coordinate attacks, promise support, or bluff.
* **Simultaneous Orders:** Everyone writes orders at the same time (move, hold, support, convoy).
* **Resolution:** Orders are revealed and resolved together—so outcomes depend heavily on coordination and prediction, not reflexes or dice.
* **Retreats:** Dislodged units may retreat to a legal adjacent space or be removed.
* **Winter Adjustments:** If you control more supply centers than units, you build; if fewer, you disband.

### Key mechanics in one paragraph

There’s **no randomness** (no dice/cards). Success comes from reading people, forming coalitions, and timing betrayals. Units have simple movement rules, but the interactions—especially **support** (adding strength) and **convoys** (fleets transporting armies across water)—create a deep tactical layer where diplomacy and tactics constantly shape each other.

### Why it’s famous

Diplomacy is known for being a “social strategy” game: the board is only half the battle—the other half is convincing other humans to help you, or at least not stop you, long enough to win.
